I — 1760–1840
Art & Aesthetics

The Sublime of Smoke & Steam

Romanticism rose in direct tension with industrialisation. Artists like Turner and Constable rendered the landscape with turbulent force — chimneys belching smoke into sunsets, railways tearing through pastoral calm. The "Sublime" was both awe and dread of the machine age. Working-class realism emerged later, portraying factory labourers with unflinching dignity.

After Turner — Industrial Sublime, c.1830. Smoke meets sunset over the mill towns.

Socio-Economic Context

Capital, Labour & the New Proletariat

The enclosure of common land drove rural populations into the new factory cities. A new class structure crystallised — industrial capitalists amassing unprecedented wealth while waged labourers toiled 14-hour days. The Corn Laws kept bread expensive for the poor. Luddite rebellions and early trade unionism showed that the economic rupture was also a cultural one. GDP growth was unprecedented; life expectancy in Manchester fell.

Key Shift
Agrarian → industrial wage economy. Labour commodified for the first time at mass scale.
Art Market
Emerging bourgeois patronage replaced aristocratic commissions. Landscape & genre painting democratised collecting.
Technology of the Age

Steam, Steel & the Mechanical Loom

Watt's steam engine (1769) was the era's defining technology. The power loom displaced handweavers; the spinning jenny concentrated textile production. Iron-framed buildings and railway networks reshaped geography. The telegraph would follow, shrinking time and space for commerce. Printing presses spread ideas — and propaganda — faster than ever before.

II — 1870–1914
Art & Aesthetics

Impressionism, Excess & the Birth of the Avant-Garde

Extreme wealth produced extreme culture. In Paris, the Belle Époque saw Impressionism shatter academic tradition — Monet's luminous surfaces, Toulouse-Lautrec's cabaret posters, Klimt's gilded decadence. In America, the Gilded Age's robber barons built palatial museums to legitimise new money. Simultaneously, socialist realists and the Arts & Crafts movement protested industrial ugliness.

After Monet — Water Lilies series, c.1906. Impressionist dissolution of form.

Socio-Economic Context

Monopoly Capital, Empire & the Department Store

Standard Oil, Carnegie Steel, railroad trusts — a handful of men commanded wealth comparable to nation-states. The era invented modern consumer culture: the department store (Bon Marché, Harrods) turned shopping into spectacle. Imperialist expansion funded European prosperity while exporting exploitation globally. The Paris Commune of 1871 showed the explosive fault lines beneath the glittering surface. Art was luxury goods, cultural capital, and subversion simultaneously.

Key Shift
Finance capitalism displaces industrial capitalism. Stocks, bonds & speculation create paper fortunes.
Art Market
Dealer-gallery system emerges. Durand-Ruel stakes his fortune on the Impressionists — and wins.
Technology of the Age

Electricity, the Camera & the Age of Reproduction

Edison's electric light, the telephone, and early cinema transformed daily life and the economics of spectacle. Photography's rise existentially challenged painting — liberating it, many argue, into abstraction and expression. The mass-produced print (litho poster, illustrated magazine) turned art into advertising. Steel-frame skyscrapers and the Eiffel Tower announced a new material sublime.

III — 2000–Present
Art & Aesthetics

Hyperrealism, Meme Culture & the Neural Canvas

Postmodern irony gave way to digital sincerity — then to the attention economy's frenetic aesthetics. Street art entered auction houses; NFTs briefly made scarcity from infinity. AI image generation (Midjourney, DALL·E, Stable Diffusion) democratised visual production at scale, raising urgent questions about authorship, labour, and what it means to make something. The artist-algorithm collaboration is the defining creative question of the decade.

Human-AI collaboration — the new creative dyad. Who holds the brush?

Socio-Economic Context

Platform Capitalism, Attention Markets & Creative Precarity

Platforms like Instagram and TikTok turned every person into a content producer, yet concentrated revenue in a handful of companies. The "creator economy" promised independence but delivered algorithmic precarity. Meanwhile, generative AI tools commoditise previously skilled creative work — compressing time-to-image from weeks to seconds. The economic question: when art can be generated on demand, what is the value of the artist's labour?

Key Shift
Cognitive capitalism — knowledge, creativity, and attention become the primary raw materials of economic value.
Art Market
Blue-chip art as an asset class. AI-generated works enter auction. Beeple's NFT sold for $69M in 2021.
Technology of the Age

Diffusion Models, LLMs & the Generative Turn

Neural networks trained on billions of images can now synthesise any visual from a text prompt. Transformer architectures (GPT, Claude) do the same for language. The creative workflow has been inverted: the constraint is no longer technical execution but imagination and editorial judgment. Humans curate, prompt, iterate, and contextualise — the algorithm renders.

Human–AI Creative Collaboration

The Present Mode

Generative amplification

Artists use AI to rapidly explore variations, styles, and compositions that would take months by hand. The human provides intent and taste; the model provides exhaustive possibility space. Artists like Holly Herndon and Refik Anadol have made this partnership central to their practice.

Curation as authorship

When generation is instant, the artist's eye — the judgment of what to keep, what to reject, what to combine — becomes the primary creative act. Marcel Duchamp's readymades anticipated this: the selection and framing of an object as the creative gesture.

Economic disruption & new models

Stock photography, junior illustration, concept art — entire income strata have been compressed by generative tools. Yet new revenue models emerge: prompt engineering, AI art direction, dataset curation, and the irreducible value of live human performance and provenance.
